Technical Chart Reading

A visual reference. TA reads probability from crowd behavior — it manages risk, it does not predict.

1. The candlestick — the atom of a chart

Every candle shows four prices over one time period (1 min, 1 hour, 1 day...): where it Opened, the High, the Low, and where it Closed. The thick part is the body (open→close); the thin lines are wicks (or shadows), showing the extremes price reached and got rejected from.

Bullish (often green/hollow): closed higher than it opened — buyers won the period.
Bearish (often red/filled): closed lower than it opened — sellers won.

Wicks tell the story: a long lower wick = price dropped but buyers pushed it back up (rejection of lows). A long upper wick = sellers rejected higher prices. Bodies show conviction; wicks show the fight.

2. Trend structure — the first thing to read

Before anything else, answer one question: is price making higher highs or lower lows? Trend is just the pattern of swing points.

Uptrend: Higher Highs + Higher Lows. Bias = look for buys.
Downtrend: Lower Highs + Lower Lows. Bias = look for sells.
Range: flat highs and lows, price bouncing sideways.

Trend breaks when the pattern fails — e.g. an uptrend making its first lower low is an early warning the trend is turning.

3. Support & resistance — where the crowd acts

Support = a price floor where buyers repeatedly step in. Resistance = a ceiling where sellers repeatedly appear. These are zones, not exact lines. The more times a level is tested and holds, the more traders watch it — which is what makes it matter.

Role reversal: once broken, support becomes resistance and vice-versa — an old floor becomes the new ceiling. This "flip" is one of the most reliable ideas in charting.

Why it works: levels are memory. Traders who bought at support defend it; traders trapped on the wrong side sell at breakeven when price returns.

4. High-signal candles

Doji — indecision

Open ≈ close, tiny body, wicks both sides. Neither side won. At the top of a run or bottom of a drop, it warns momentum is stalling.

Hammer / Pin bar — rejection

Small body, long wick. Long lower wick at a low = buyers rejected lower prices (bullish). Long upper wick at a high (shooting star) = sellers rejected higher prices (bearish).

Engulfing — takeover

A candle whose body fully swallows the prior candle's body. Bullish engulfing at a low / bearish engulfing at a high signals control changing hands.

The rule that matters

A signal candle only counts at a level (support, resistance, trendline). A hammer in the middle of nowhere is noise. Location + candle = signal.

5. Chart patterns — structure over many candles

  • Double top / bottom (M / W): price fails twice at the same level → reversal. Confirmed when it breaks the middle "neckline."
  • Head & shoulders: three peaks, middle highest → topping pattern. Inverse version = bottoming. Trigger = neckline break.
  • Triangles: price coils into a narrowing range (ascending = bullish bias, descending = bearish, symmetrical = neutral). Trade the breakout direction.
  • Flags & pennants: a sharp move, then a small consolidation, then continuation in the same direction. Continuation patterns, not reversals.
Universal principle: patterns are only "confirmed" on the break and close beyond their boundary — never on a wick poking through. Anticipating the break is guessing; trading the confirmed break is reading.

6. Indicators — supporting evidence, not the story

IndicatorWhat it showsCommon read
Moving Average (MA)Average price over N candles — smooths trend. 20/50/200 common.Price above rising MA = uptrend. 50 crossing above 200 = "golden cross"; below = "death cross."
RSI (0–100)Momentum / speed of moves. Default 14.>70 = overbought, <30 = oversold. Divergence (price new high, RSI lower high) warns of exhaustion.
MACDDifference between two MAs — momentum & direction.MACD line crossing its signal line = momentum shift. Histogram shrinking = momentum fading.
VolumeHow much traded. Conviction behind a move.Breakouts on high volume are trustworthy; on low volume, suspect a fake-out.
Trap to avoid: indicators are derived from price — they lag it. Stacking ten of them doesn't give ten confirmations; they mostly say the same thing. Price action leads; use 1–2 indicators as a second opinion, not the whole thesis.

7. Timeframes & confluence

Higher timeframes dominate. A daily-chart resistance outranks a 5-minute one. The pros read top-down: find the trend and key levels on the daily/4-hour, then drop to a lower timeframe (15-min/5-min) to time the entry.

Confluence = your edge. One signal is weak. Take a trade when several stack in the same place: e.g. price hits a daily support and the 200-MA and RSI is oversold and a bullish engulfing prints. Each factor alone is a coin-flip; together they tilt the odds and give you a tight, well-defined stop.

8. Reading a chart, step by step

  • 1. Zoom out. Trend on the higher timeframe: up, down, or ranging?
  • 2. Mark levels. Draw the obvious support/resistance zones and trendlines. Fewer, cleaner lines beat a cluttered chart.
  • 3. Where is price now relative to those levels? Approaching one, or in no-man's-land? (No-man's-land = no trade.)
  • 4. Wait for a trigger at a level: a signal candle, a confirmed break, a retest.
  • 5. Define invalidation first. Where does this idea prove wrong? That's your stop. If the stop is far, the trade is bad — skip it.
  • 6. Check reward vs risk. Target at least 2× your risk to the next level. No 2:1, no trade.
What TA can and cannot do. It cannot predict the next candle — anyone who says otherwise is guessing with confidence. What it does: define where to act, where you're wrong (the stop), and whether the payoff justifies the risk. Two traders can read the same chart perfectly and one still loses — because the edge is in risk management and doing it 100 times, not in calling any single move.
